Color Blindness Problem

1.Introduction

The usability problem with traffic light for colorblind people is that they cannot easily distinguish between red and green traffic lights, which can lead to confusion and dangerous situations. Color blindness can make it difficult or impossible to distinguish between certain colors, such as red and green. This can confuse people who rely on color as a visual cue to understand traffic light status. If you do not know the color of the traffic light, it is easy to make a mistake when entering an intersection, increasing the risk of an accident. Current traffic light designs do not provide an accessible way for people with color blindness to determine traffic light status. This can create accessibility issues and prevent these individuals from using the system effectively. Different intersections may use slightly different shades and patterns for traffic lights, which can be confusing for colorblind people accustomed to certain designs.

2.Context of use+Main Audience+Empiric Laws

Context of use

To address this problem, an app designed for people with color blindness can help them to navigate traffic more safely and confidently. The app can use the camera on the user's smartphone to detect traffic signals and then provide them with a color-coded interpretation that they can easily understand. The app can also use audio cues to alert the user when a traffic signal changes, which can be particularly useful for those who are visually impaired. By providing people with color blindness with a reliable tool to interpret traffic signals, this app can help them to travel more independently and confidently, reducing the risk of accidents and improving their overall quality of life.

Main Audience

The main audience for an app that helps people with color blindness in traffic would be individuals who have been diagnosed with some form of color blindness, particularly those who struggle with distinguishing between red and green. This could include people of all ages, from teenagers learning to drive to elderly individuals who rely on public transportation to get around.

In particular, the app could be helpful for individuals who live in urban areas with high levels of traffic, as well as for those who frequently travel to unfamiliar places where they may not be familiar with the layout of the streets or the timing of traffic signals. The app could also be beneficial for people who have jobs that require them to drive or spend a lot of time on the road, such as truck drivers or delivery personnel.

Moreover, the app could be used by family members or caregivers of individuals with color blindness, who may be concerned about their safety when navigating traffic. By providing a reliable and easy-to-use tool to interpret traffic signals, the app can help give peace of mind to those who care for people with color blindness. Overall, the app could be a valuable resource for anyone with color blindness who wants to travel more safely and confidently in traffic.

Empiric Laws

Fitts' Law

This law states that the time required to move to a target area is a function of the distance to the target and the size of the target. In the case of traffic lights, the size and placement of the lights can affect how quickly and accurately colorblind individuals can perceive them.

Hick's Law

This law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases as the number of choices increases. This can be applied to the design of traffic lights, as having too many options or visual distractions can make it harder for colorblind individuals to distinguish between the red and green lights.

Steering Law

This law states that the time it takes to make a steering movement is a function of the size of the target and the angle between the starting position and the target. This can be applied to the design of traffic lights to ensure that the lights are easily visible and distinguishable from different angles and distances.
